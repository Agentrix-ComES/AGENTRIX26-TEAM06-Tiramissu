# Scam Alerts in Sri Lanka

## Gem and Jewellery Scams
One of the most notorious scams in Sri Lanka involves fake or highly over-priced gems. Tourists may be approached by friendly locals offering to take them to a "gem exhibition" or a "wholesale dealer." The stones sold are often glass or inferior gems cut to look valuable. Never buy gems from unsolicited guides or street touts. If you wish to purchase jewellery, only go to reputable, government-approved shops and always ask for a certificate of authenticity.

## The Spice Garden Scam
While visiting a spice garden can be educational, many around tourist hubs (like Kandy and Matale) operate as high-pressure tourist traps. Guides will give a free tour and then aggressively push overpriced "Ayurvedic" medicines and spices. Often, these products are diluted or poor quality, sold at 10 to 20 times the market price. You are under no obligation to buy anything; a small tip for the guide is sufficient. 

## The "Special Festival" or "Closed Temple" Scam
In cities like Colombo or Kandy, a friendly stranger might approach you and say the temple or attraction you are heading to is closed for a "special Buddhist holiday" or "cleaning." They will then offer to take you to another attraction, which usually involves a visit to a gem shop or a souvenir store where they receive a commission. Always verify opening times yourself at the official entrance.

## The Stilt Fishermen Photo Scam
Along the southern coast, you will often see traditional "stilt fishermen." However, many of these men are not fishing; they are actors waiting for tourists. If you take a photo, they or their associates will aggressively demand a fee (often 500 LKR or more). If you want a photo, agree on the price beforehand to avoid an unpleasant confrontation.

## Tuk-Tuk Overcharging
Tuk-tuk drivers hanging around hotels and major tourist spots often quote inflated prices. Some might agree to a low price initially but then claim the price was "per person" upon reaching the destination. Always clarify that the negotiated fare is for the total journey. Whenever possible, use ride-hailing apps like PickMe or Uber, which provide transparent, metered pricing.
